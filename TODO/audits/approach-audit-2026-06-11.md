### strong-authentication — 3 MAJOR, 3 MINOR, 2 SUGGESTION

- **[MAJOR] supported_qualities_notes: compliance** — The note claims GDPR 'requires multi-factor or phishing-resistant authentication for privileged access.' GDPR Art. 32 only mandates 'appropriate technical and organisational measures' and never names MFA. NIST SP 800-63 is also guidance, not a regulatory framework — only PCI DSS in the list genuinely mandates MFA. As written this is a factually wrong compliance claim on a page architects may cite in reviews.
  - *Suggestion:* Rewrite to: PCI DSS mandates MFA for access to cardholder data environments; NIST SP 800-63B defines the assurance levels (AAL2/AAL3) that regulations and contracts increasingly reference. Drop GDPR or recast it as 'regulators interpret GDPR's appropriate-measures duty as expecting MFA for sensitive processing.'
- **[MAJOR] intent** — Intent promises assurance 'that credential-based attacks (phishing, credential stuffing, brute force) cannot succeed.' The page's own Failure Modes section contradicts this (MFA fatigue, password-only fallback, stolen-token abuse). Stating absolute prevention as the intent is misleading and internally inconsistent.
  - *Suggestion:* Calibrate: 'so that stolen or guessed credentials alone are insufficient to gain access' — raises the bar without claiming attacks cannot succeed.
- **[MAJOR] ## Variants and Related Tactics / ## Verification** — 'making phishing structurally impossible' and the verification item 'verify that FIDO2/WebAuthn users cannot be phished' state a conditional property as universal. Origin binding does defeat credential-harvesting pages, but users remain phishable whenever a weaker fallback factor (TOTP, SMS, recovery flow) is still enrolled, and adversary-in-the-middle kits steal post-auth session tokens regardless of the login factor.
  - *Suggestion:* Scope the claim: 'origin binding makes the WebAuthn ceremony itself unphishable; the protection holds only if weaker fallback factors are removed.' Make the verification item check exactly that: phishing simulation succeeds 0 times against accounts with no fallback factor enrolled.
- **[MINOR] ## References** — ASVS citation says 'V2 (Authentication)', which is ASVS 4.x chapter numbering; ASVS 5.0 (released May 2025) renumbered Authentication to V6. The FIDO entry links a FIDO Alliance landing page rather than the actual specification — WebAuthn is a W3C Recommendation, so the durable, authoritative source is the W3C TR page. (The V2 numbering issue also appears on secret-management.md, which cites 'V2.10 (Service Authentication)' — same stale-version pattern sitewide.)
  - *Suggestion:* Cite 'Authentication chapter' without a version-bound number or update to ASVS 5.0's V6; replace the FIDO Alliance landing page with https://www.w3.org/TR/webauthn-3/.
- **[MINOR] tradeoff_notes: usability** — 'add friction to every login and can frustrate users' names the cost but the consequence is the generic 'frustration' — close to the 'adds complexity'-class warning the template forbids. The most concrete usability cost of MFA is missing entirely: lost or replaced second factors lock users out, and the account-recovery/helpdesk-reset path becomes both a support burden and the new weakest link attackers target.
  - *Suggestion:* Add the recovery cost: lost-device lockouts, helpdesk reset volume, and recovery flows that attackers socially engineer — with the condition (bites hardest for large consumer user bases and hardware-key deployments).
- **[MINOR] applicability** — The applicability paragraph drifts into mechanism territory: 'Service-to-service authentication should use mutual TLS or signed JWT tokens, not shared secrets' restates content already in mechanism and in How It Works (bullet 5), so the same advice appears three times while the 'skip when' side stays thin ('low-risk, non-sensitive contexts' is the only boundary given).
  - *Suggestion:* Cut the service-to-service sentence from applicability and spend the words on the skip side, e.g. anonymous/public read-only systems or kiosk contexts where identity differentiation adds no value.
- **[SUGGESTION] supported_qualities: non-repudiation** — The non-repudiation mapping is tenuous as argued: authentication alone does not yield non-repudiation — that additionally requires tamper-evident audit trails or signatures binding the verified identity to specific actions. The note's hedge ('making it difficult ... to deny') implicitly concedes this, and it overlaps heavily with the accountability note (both reduce to 'actions tie to a verified identity').
  - *Suggestion:* Either drop non-repudiation or sharpen the note to name the missing precondition: 'a necessary input to non-repudiation — effective only when combined with tamper-evident audit logging of the authenticated actions.'
- **[SUGGESTION] ## Verification** — The brute-force item mixes two numbers ambiguously: 'send 100 failed login attempts ... verify rate limiting or account lockout engages within the configured threshold (for example after 5 attempts)' — if lockout triggers at 5, the 100 attempts are irrelevant; the pass/fail condition is stated indirectly.
  - *Suggestion:* State it as a single assertion: 'after N failed attempts (e.g. 5), the next attempt with the correct password is also rejected until lockout expires.'

### api-gateway — 1 MAJOR, 5 MINOR, 1 SUGGESTION

- **[MAJOR] References** — RFC 6749 is described as a "token-based authentication and delegation model enforced at the gateway". OAuth 2.0 is explicitly an authorization/delegation framework, not an authentication protocol — using it as authentication is a well-known security anti-pattern (OIDC exists precisely to add the authentication layer). On a page tagged `secure` this conflation is misleading.
  - *Suggestion:* Describe RFC 6749 as the authorization/delegation framework whose access tokens the gateway validates, or reference OpenID Connect alongside it when authentication is meant.
- **[MINOR] supported_qualities_notes (availability)** — The note claims the gateway "fails over transparently via health-check routing and retry-with-jitter, so transient backend failures stay hidden from callers" — stated as universal, yet the body's Single Point of Failure mode says the opposite holds unless the gateway fleet itself is redundant. Also, gateway-level retries silently duplicate non-idempotent requests; "stay hidden from callers" only holds for idempotent calls.
  - *Suggestion:* Condition the claim: availability improves for backend failures provided the gateway fleet is redundant, and retries are safe only for idempotent routes.
- **[MINOR] related_requirements_notes (production-anomalies-detectable-within-2-minutes)** — "Single pane of glass" is a marketing cliché the voice rules exclude, and "detect anomalies within seconds" overclaims: the gateway emits telemetry; detection latency depends on the downstream alerting pipeline, not the gateway.
  - *Suggestion:* State the actual contribution: uniform per-route logs and metrics give the monitoring stack a complete signal source, making the 2-minute detection budget achievable.
- **[MINOR] related_requirements_notes (withstand-ddos-attack)** — The note implies the gateway withstands DDoS via connection limits and IP filtering. Gateway-level controls address application-layer (L7) DoS; volumetric attacks saturate bandwidth upstream of the gateway, which is why the note has to lean on "integration with upstream WAF/CDN" — the conditional scope should be explicit rather than implied.
  - *Suggestion:* Scope the claim to L7 attacks and name the upstream CDN/scrubbing dependency as a precondition for volumetric attacks.
- **[MINOR] ## Verification** — The End-to-End Latency Overhead item ends with tuning commentary ("Note that p99 spikes usually come from synchronous plugin execution... profiling per-plugin latency contribution is essential") — diagnostic advice, not a measurable signal, and "Note that" / "is essential" are filler/prescriptive phrasing.   - *Suggestion:* Cut the commentary or move the synchronous-plugin insight into Failure Modes (Latency Amplification already covers it).
- **[MINOR] supported_qualities_notes (scalability) / related_requirements_notes (handle-sudden-increase-in-traffic)** — Both notes credit "request buffering, connection pooling, and (tight) integration with autoscaling" for absorbing spikes — near-identical wording across two notes, and overstated: buffering absorbs only brief bursts; sustained spikes require shedding (429) or backend scaling, which the gateway signals but does not perform. "Tight integration with autoscaling" is vague.
  - *Suggestion:* Differentiate the notes: scalability = offloading cross-cutting work from backends; traffic-spike requirement = admission control sheds excess load so backends scale without cascading overload.

  - *Suggestion:* Tighten intent to the motivation alone, e.g. "Concentrate cross-cutting edge concerns in one managed entry point so backend services stay focused on business logic", and let mechanism own the list.

### cqrs — 1 MAJOR, 5 MINOR, 2 SUGGESTION

- **[MAJOR] How It Works** — The claim 'a new read model can be built from scratch by replaying the event or change stream' is stated as a universal CQRS property, but it only holds with event sourcing or a fully retained change log. CDC streams from a mutable write store typically retain limited history, so rebuilds there come from a snapshot plus reprojection. The page itself lists Event Sourcing as a separate variant, contradicting this claim.
  - *Suggestion:* Condition the claim: 'where the change stream is durable and complete (e.g. event sourcing or a retained log), a new read model can be rebuilt by replay; otherwise rebuilds reproject from the current write-store state.'
- **[MINOR] Verification** — The rebuild test requires the replay to produce 'a byte-identical result.' Real projections embed processing timestamps, interleave events non-deterministically across partitions, and differ in storage internals, so byte equality is a check that would routinely fail even on a healthy system. It also presupposes 'the event stream' exists, which plain (non-event-sourced) CQRS lacks.
  - *Suggestion:* Assert logical equivalence instead: row counts and a content checksum over business fields match the live view, or a record-level diff returns zero discrepancies.
- **[MINOR] tradeoff_notes** — The `observability` note ('Monitoring projection lag, data drift, and consumer health is critical for maintaining trust in the read models') states an operational imperative, not a cost and its consequence. It reads as advice, and largely overlaps the `operability` note ('consumer lag dashboards').
  - *Suggestion:* Name the cost and trigger, e.g.: 'Each projector adds lag, drift, and dead-letter metrics the team must build and watch; without them, stale or corrupted views surface only as user-reported data loss.' Or fold the point into the operability note and drop the observability tradeoff.
- **[MINOR] Failure Modes** — Two list items are not observable failure conditions: 'Storage overhead: ... significantly increases data storage costs' is a cost that belongs in tradeoff_notes, and 'On a simple CRUD domain, the projection plumbing ... adds maintenance burden' is a misapplication note that duplicates the `applicability` field. A stray blank line between lines 45 and 47 also splits the bullet list in two.
  - *Suggestion:* Move the storage-cost point into a tradeoff note, delete the CRUD item (applicability already covers it), and rejoin the list.
- **[MINOR] How It Works** — Bullet 4 makes the same point twice ('typically runs asynchronously ... allowing the write side to return quickly' followed by 'While often asynchronous for scale, CQRS can also be implemented synchronously ...'), and the second sentence is passive ('can be implemented', 'is prioritized').
  - *Suggestion:* Merge into one active sentence: 'The projection step usually runs asynchronously, decoupling write latency from view-update cost; teams that need read-your-writes consistency project synchronously inside the write transaction and accept the throughput cost.'
- **[MINOR] Verification** — The freshness SLI is circular: '99.9% of queries reflect state changes within the p95 projection lag threshold' defines its bound in terms of the lag SLO from the first item, so it is unclear what independent signal it adds or how 'during automated verification runs' is measured.
  - *Suggestion:* Give it an independent absolute bound, e.g. '99.9% of queries return data no older than 5 s, measured by a synthetic write-then-read probe.'
- **[SUGGESTION] Overview** — Minor voice issues: passive 'Queries are served entirely from the read models' (How It Works bullet 3), the vague intensifier 'significantly' appears in both `applicability` and Failure Modes, and the bold emphasis on '**asynchronous**' in the overview is decorative.
  - *Suggestion:* Use 'The read models serve all queries', replace 'significantly' with the concrete dimension (e.g. 'by an order of magnitude in volume'), and drop the bold.
- **[SUGGESTION] References** — The page has no References section even though CQRS has canonical, durable, vendor-neutral sources, which would strengthen the page's authority.
  - *Suggestion:* Consider adding Greg Young's 'CQRS Documents', Martin Fowler's CQRS bliki entry, and/or Vernon's 'Implementing Domain-Driven Design', linked to /references/ anchors where available.

- **[MINOR] ## How It Works / ## Failure Modes** — Voice slips: 'keyed by IP address, account, API key, tenant, route, or operation cost as appropriate' (mechanism field) ends in hedging filler, and the failure-mode bullet 'Limiter-state failure is handled poorly' opens in passive voice where the other bullets state an observable condition with an actor.
  - *Suggestion:* Drop 'as appropriate'; rephrase the bullet actively, e.g. 'The limiter loses its counter store and fails open: protection disappears exactly when traffic is high; fail-closed on all endpoints creates a self-inflicted outage.'
- **[SUGGESTION] tradeoff_notes (operability)** — The note names concrete tasks (per-route tuning, tenant exceptions, incident overrides) but the consequence — 'require continuous operational ownership' — is soft; it never says what breaks when that ownership lapses.
  - *Suggestion:* Add the bite: budgets drift from real traffic patterns over time, producing false throttling of legitimate users or silent under-protection until someone retunes them.

### saga-pattern — 6 MINOR, 1 SUGGESTION

- **[MINOR] How It Works / mechanism** — Retriable transactions are described as "guaranteed to eventually succeed through retries" (and "guaranteed to succeed" in the mechanism field). The guarantee is a design obligation, not an inherent property — retries alone guarantee nothing; the designer must choose only operations that cannot fail for business reasons. As written it can mislead readers into placing fallible steps after the pivot.
  - *Suggestion:* Rephrase as a constraint, e.g. "steps after the pivot must be designed so they cannot fail for business reasons, so retries eventually succeed."
- **[MINOR] Failure Modes (Sequencing Design Errors)** — The bullet says "If the Saga later fails and rolls back", contradicting the page's own careful framing that sagas compensate rather than roll back (overview: "not an ACID rollback"). It also states irreversible steps "must always be ... placed after the pivot", overlooking that the irreversible action is typically the pivot itself. "resulting in damage control" is vague filler.
  - *Suggestion:* Use "compensates" instead of "rolls back", allow "as the pivot or after it", and name the concrete consequence (e.g. an email or payout that cannot be retracted).
- **[MINOR] Failure Modes (Lack of Isolation) / tradeoff_notes.consistency** — The countermeasures sentence (semantic locks, commutative updates, pessimistic re-reads) repeats tradeoff_notes.consistency nearly verbatim, and is indented as a continuation of the Non-repeatable Reads sub-bullet although it applies to all three anomalies.
  - *Suggestion:* Keep the countermeasure list in one place (the body), let the tradeoff note state cost and consequence only, and promote the countermeasures line out of the sub-bullet nesting.
- **[MINOR] supported_qualities_notes.availability** — "ensuring services stay responsive during multi-step flows" overclaims: sagas remove one blocking cause (long-held locks, in-doubt 2PC participants); they do not ensure responsiveness.
  - *Suggestion:* Soften to "so services stay responsive" or "services keep serving requests during multi-step flows".
- **[MINOR] intent** — The intent field carries the mechanism ("by splitting a long-running business transaction into a sequence of local transactions, compensating already completed steps if a failure occurs"), duplicating the mechanism field rather than stating only the why.
  - *Suggestion:* Trim intent to the goal, e.g. "Reach a consistent business outcome across services where a distributed ACID transaction is impractical."
- **[MINOR] How It Works** — Passive constructions cluster against the active-voice rule: "transactions are classified", "they are undone using compensating actions", "Compensations must be continuously retried", "messages and commands will be redelivered".
  - *Suggestion:* Recast with the actor as subject, e.g. "The saga undoes them via compensating actions", "The broker redelivers messages", "The orchestrator retries compensations".
- **[SUGGESTION] supported_qualities** — The intent literally promises "eventual data consistency" and a quality slug eventual-consistency exists but is not mapped; applicability also argues a scalability advantage over 2PC, and scalability exists as a quality. Both look like plausible missing mappings.
  - *Suggestion:* Consider adding eventual-consistency (arguably the most precise quality for this pattern) and weigh scalability; keep the list curated rather than exhaustive.

### self-contained-systems — 4 MINOR, 1 SUGGESTION

- **[MINOR] supported_qualities_notes (autonomy)** — The autonomy note states unconditionally that each system 'keeps serving users while neighboring systems are down.' That holds only when integration is genuinely async/replicated — the body itself admits synchronous calls exist as 'the rare exception,' so the claim is conditional, not universal.
  - *Suggestion:* Tie the claim to its precondition, e.g. '…and, with async replication in place, keeps serving users while neighbors are down.'
- **[MINOR] supported_qualities_notes (deployability)** — 'Coordinated big-bang releases disappear by construction' overstates. Breaking changes to shared event schemas or link contracts between systems still force coordinated rollouts (expand/contract sequencing); independent deployment is the default, not a structural guarantee.
  - *Suggestion:* Soften to 'routine releases need no cross-system coordination' or note that only contract-breaking changes require sequencing.
- **[MINOR] ## Verification** — The change-coupling item ('recurring cross-system features flag a wrong cut') names a signal but no threshold — 'recurring' is undefined, so the check has no pass/fail line. The other three items are genuinely measurable.
  - *Suggestion:* Add a concrete bound, e.g. 'more than ~20% of features touching two or more systems over a quarter flags a wrong cut.'
- **[MINOR] mechanism** — The closing clause 'no shared runtime, no shared database, shared business code avoided' ends in a passive fragment ('avoided'), against the active-voice rule, and slightly duplicates the loose-coupling note's 'no shared database' phrasing.
  - *Suggestion:* Make it active and parallel: 'no shared runtime, no shared database, and teams avoid shared business code.'
- **[SUGGESTION] supported_qualities** — Failure independence is one of the page's strongest arguments (autonomy note, the 'distributed monolith' failure mode, the chaos check in Verification), yet no reliability-family quality appears in supported_qualities. fault-isolation and resilience both exist as quality slugs.
  - *Suggestion:* Consider adding fault-isolation (or resilience) with a note like 'Async-only integration confines a system's outage to its own domain; neighbors keep serving from replicated data.'

### standby-failover — 3 MINOR, 2 SUGGESTION

- **[MINOR] body overview (second paragraph)** — "A hot spare processes the same inputs in parallel and fails over in milliseconds" states a conditional outcome as universal. End-to-end failover time is usually dominated by failure detection — heartbeat or health-probe timeouts commonly run seconds — so millisecond failover only holds with lease/connection-level detection or active-active routing, not for hot spares in general.
  - *Suggestion:* Soften to e.g. "takes over near-instantly once failure is detected" or note that detection time, not promotion, dominates the recovery clock.
- **[MINOR] ## Failure Modes** — The split-brain bullet claims "A quorum or fencing token prevents it" — flat "prevents" is too strong and conflates two different mechanisms: quorum prevents two nodes from both becoming active, while fencing prevents an already-deposed active from corrupting shared state. It is also the only bullet that mixes a mitigation into a section otherwise reserved for observable failures.
  - *Suggestion:* Either drop the mitigation clause (it belongs in How It Works or Variants) or split the roles: "quorum keeps a partition from electing two actives; fencing stops a deposed active from writing."
- **[MINOR] mechanism** — The hot-to-cold cost-vs-recovery-time spectrum is stated three times in near-identical words: mechanism ("Standbys range from hot ... trading cost against recovery time"), body paragraph 2 ("Warmer means faster recovery at higher standing cost"), and tradeoff_notes.cost ("colder standbys cut that cost but lengthen recovery time"). The frontmatter fields and body should each do distinct work.
  - *Suggestion:* Trim the spectrum clause from `mechanism` (keep detector → promote → rejoin) and let body paragraph 2 own the temperature spectrum; the cost trade-off note can keep its instance since it argues the consequence.
- **[SUGGESTION] ## References** — No References section, yet the aka terms (Active Redundancy, Passive Redundancy, Hot/Warm/Cold Spare) come straight from the Bass et al. availability-tactics taxonomy, and the site's references page already has an anchor for it (/references/#bass2021software, used by other pages).
  - *Suggestion:* Add a References section citing Bass et al., Software Architecture in Practice, with the existing /references/#bass2021software anchor; mentioning the Bass terminology lineage in body prose would also justify the six aliases.
- **[SUGGESTION] supported_qualities** — `reliability` exists as a quality slug and plausibly follows from the mechanism (a redundant peer raises system-level continuity of service / effective MTBF), yet only availability, fault-tolerance, and recoverability are mapped. Possibly an intentional curation choice, but worth a look.
  - *Suggestion:* Consider whether `reliability` (and/or `resilience`) deserves a supported_qualities entry with a note arguing the redundancy-to-continuity link; omit if the team treats those as covered by availability/fault-tolerance.

## aka collisions (near-misses)

- **Standby/Failover aka "Redundancy" / "Active Redundancy"** vs. **N-Version Redundancy**: bare "Redundancy" is too broad and could equally mean N-version voting; in Bass, *active redundancy* means parallel processing — closer to voting than to a passive spare. Propose dropping "Redundancy" or scoping it.
- **Rate Limiting aka "Throttling"** vs. **Limit Event Response**: "Throttling" is also the standard name for the LER tactic; readers from Bass would expect it there. Decide one owner.
- **Manage Event Arrival aka "Service-Level Agreement"**: SLA is a far broader concept; as an index term it misleads (readers expect availability/SLO content). Propose removal or rewording ("Arrival-Rate SLA").
- **Watchdog Supervision aka "Monitor"**: extremely generic; collides conceptually with observability/monitoring. Propose "Watchdog Timer" or drop.
- **Sidecar aka "Ambassador"**: Ambassador is usually treated as a distinct sub-pattern (outbound proxy). Defensible as an index term; flag only.
- **Open Host Service aka "Published Language"**: a separate DDD pattern in its own right; acceptable since the page covers both, but worth a conscious decision.

## Terminology inconsistencies

- **"backpressure" vs "back-pressure"**: rate-limiting.md uses `backpressure`; bulkheads, cqrs, limit-event-response, manage-event-arrival use `back-pressure`. Unify (suggest unhyphenated, the prevailing industry spelling).
- **upstream/downstream direction flips**: OHS uses DDD convention (downstream = consumer of the host); BFF, bulkheads, rate-limiting, asynchronous-messaging use call-path convention (downstream = services you call). Both are defensible in context, but BFF's "downstream services" are OHS's "upstream context". Suggest a one-line gloss on the OHS page rather than global rewording.
- **"Skip when/with/for" (19 files) vs "Avoid when/for/only" (16 files)** in applicability: clearly two authoring generations. Cosmetic; unify to one verb if you touch pages anyway.
- **consumer vs client**: messaging/EDA pages say "consumer" (broker semantics), gateway/BFF say "client" — these are genuinely different roles; no change recommended, just noted as deliberate.
